Standard Error and Confidence Intervals for the Slope

Measuring slope uncertainty and building a plausible range for the true slope
Sample slope vs. population slope

In simple linear regression, the sample slope is our estimate of the unknown population slope .

Because of sampling variability, different random samples would give fitted slopes. The standard error, SE(), measures the typical of those sample slopes.

A confidence combines the slope estimate, its SE, and a multiplier to give a plausible for the true slope.

Different random samples produce different fitted slopes. SE(b₁) quantifies that spread.
Standard error of the slope
SE(b1)  =  s √Σ (xi − x̄)2
Here s is the residual error (RSE), and Σ(xi − x̄)2 — often called — measures the of x values.

A large SE means the slope estimate would a lot from sample to sample; a small SE means the slope estimate would vary from sample to sample.

SE(b₁) acts like a standard deviation for the slope, so we plug it into the usual ± t* × SE formula to get a confidence for β1.

In practice, R does all of this for you — summary(model) reports b₁ and SE(b₁), and confint(model) gives the CI.

If you need t* yourself: with n = 8, df = 6, t* ≈ 2.45 for a 95% CI. qt(0.975, df = 6) ⇒ 2.4469
From SE to confidence interval

Example: b₁ = 0.386 and SE = 0.118 → 0.386 ± 2.45 × 0.118 ≈ (0.097, 0.675).

The CI is centered on the slope, b₁, but it is meant to capture the true slope, β1. Across many repeated studies, about % of such intervals would capture β1.

Significance and the zero-slope test

If zero lies the 95% CI, the slope is statistically significant.

If zero lies the CI, we cannot rule out the possibility of no linear relationship.

An interval like (−0.45, −0.20) is because the whole interval is below zero. An interval like (−0.55, 0.10) is because it contains zero.

Significant: entirely below zero
Not significant: crosses zero
