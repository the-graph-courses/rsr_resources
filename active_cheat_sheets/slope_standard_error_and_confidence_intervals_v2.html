Standard Error and Confidence Intervals for the Slope

How regression measures slope uncertainty, builds a plausible range for the true slope, and checks whether zero is excluded
Slope uncertainty in one picture

In simple linear regression, the sample slope is our estimate of the unknown population slope .

Different random samples would give fitted slopes. The standard error, SE(), measures the typical of those sample slopes.

A confidence interval combines the slope estimate, its SE, and a multiplier to give a plausible range for the true slope.

The slope estimate wobbles from sample to sample. SE is the standard deviation of that wobble.
Standard error of the slope
SE(b₁) = s√Σ(xi − x̄)2
Here s is the residual standard error and Σ(xi − x̄)2 is often called Sxx.

Example: with n = 8, df = 6 and t* ≈ 2.45 for a 95% CI.
From SE to confidence interval
0.386 ± 2.45 × 0.118 gives a 95% CI of about (0.097, 0.675).

A confidence interval is centered on the slope, but it is meant to capture the true slope.

Significance and the zero-slope test

If zero lies the 95% CI, the slope is statistically significant.

If zero lies the CI, we cannot rule out no linear relationship.

An interval like (-0.45, -0.20) is significant because the whole interval is zero. An interval like (-0.55, 0.10) is not significant because it zero.
